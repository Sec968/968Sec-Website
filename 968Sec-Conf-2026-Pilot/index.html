Why 968

Built for the people who get the 2 a.m. call.

Most security conferences are watched. 968Sec is worked. Every session puts something real in front of you — a running exploit, a compromised network, a founder's tool under live fire — and asks you to engage with it directly.

Two formats, one standard: if it can't be demonstrated, it doesn't get stage time. Named for Oman's calling code, built for the Gulf's practitioners.

Format 01 — Live demo gauntlet

Hamour Tank

Designation HT/968 · Start-up proving ground

Start-ups get five minutes to demo a working cybersecurity idea, followed by five minutes of direct Q&A from a floor of practitioners. No pitch deck between you and the room. No ambiguous statistics. If it works — show it working.

The audience isn't investors being courted; it's engineers, responders, and CISOs who will use, break, or buy what you've built. Their questions are the due diligence.

The hamour is the Gulf's apex reef predator. It doesn't chase hype — it waits, watches, and strikes what's real.

Format 02 — Immersive incident labs

Scenario-based workshops

Designation WS/968 · Full-cycle incident response

Participants are dropped into realistic, already-burning environments — a ransomware-hit organisation, a poisoned supply chain, an insider mid-exfiltration — and must work the incident end to end. Not a walkthrough. Not a demo you watch. Your hands, your calls, your consequences.

Example environment: ransomware_04 — mid-encryption, backups dark, board on the phone

Phase 1

Investigate

Triage live telemetry, find patient zero, and map how far the attacker actually got — under time pressure and with incomplete logs.

Phase 2

Prioritise

Everything is on fire; not everything matters equally. Decide what to contain first, what can wait, and what you're willing to lose.

Phase 3

Restore

Bring systems back in the right order without re-introducing the threat. Recovery that reinfects the network counts as failure.

Phase 4

Explain

Defend every action to a debrief panel — the way you'd have to in front of a board, a regulator, or an insurer. If you can't explain it, you didn't understand it.

Every workshop ends with a full debrief: what the attacker actually did, what participants found and missed, and what the same decisions would have cost a real organisation.
